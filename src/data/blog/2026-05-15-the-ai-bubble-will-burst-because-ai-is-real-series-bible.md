# The AI Bubble Will Burst Because AI Is Real

## Series bible

This is the planning document for a long-form essay series.

The series is written in my first-person voice. It begins with lived experience from the dotcom bubble and uses that memory as the emotional and analytical anchor for a broader prediction about AI, capital markets, software economics and the future of companies.

The goal is not to write another generic article about whether AI is overvalued. The goal is to make a clear, grounded and provocative prediction:

> The AI bubble will not burst because AI is fake. It will burst because AI is real, and because the real future of AI undermines many of the companies currently being priced as if they own that future.

The series should feel like near-future science fiction grounded in reality. It should have the clarity of a capital markets argument, the lived texture of a first-person essay, and the scope of a book chapter about a rare technological paradigm shift.

It should avoid generic AI style. No empty futurism. No hype language. No breathless lists of buzzwords. No vague Nostradamus prophecy. If the series predicts something, it should explain the mechanism.

## Core thesis

Every major paradigm shift has a strange early phase where society can sense the power of the new thing before it understands its final form.

The market sees a shadow of the future. It then funds that shadow as if it were already a mature business model.

That is how discovery bubbles form.

The dotcom bubble was not absurd because the internet was fake. It was absurd because the market tried to price the adult internet while the internet was still a child.

Pets.com is now used as shorthand for dotcom stupidity, but online pet supplies were not permanently stupid. Chewy exists. Amazon sells pet supplies. The idea was not fake. The world around the idea was not ready.

Online clothes shopping was also once easy to mock. People said you could not try the clothes on. Now Zalando, ASOS and countless other retailers exist because logistics, payments, returns, broadband, mobile, photography, reviews, consumer trust and habit formation matured around the idea.

The mistake was not believing in the internet. The mistake was believing that every early company, business model and capital structure around the internet deserved to survive.

AI is entering the same kind of phase.

The market is not wrong to see a paradigm shift. AI is useful. It is already changing software, writing, analysis, design, customer support, education, research, media and business operations.

The market may be wrong about where value will settle.

## The central paradox

The essay series should keep returning to this paradox:

> AI is real enough to destroy the current AI trade.

That means:

- AI can be useful and the AI bubble can still burst.
- AI can transform business and OpenAI or Anthropic-style API economics can still be overvalued.
- AI can be revolutionary and SaaS companies can still be repriced downward.
- AI can increase productivity and still create massive social dislocation.
- AI can succeed and still punish investors who funded the wrong layer.

The crash, if and when it comes, will not be the death of AI. It will be the moment the market realizes it misidentified the ownership layer of the future.

## Emotional anchor

The opening should begin with the physical memory of the dotcom bubble.

I spent time at investor and founder events in San Francisco around 2000. I worked with a company that was inside that bubble. I saw people seeking capital. I saw the belief. I saw how quickly the story hardened into inevitability.

I still have two packs of business cards from people I met at those events. I kept them for posterity. Nearly every one of those companies no longer exists.

That image should open the first article.

It says:

- I was there.
- I am not looking at the dotcom bubble as an abstract chart.
- I remember the emotional texture of late-stage belief.
- I remember how real the future felt.
- I remember how much of that future was carried by companies that did not survive.

The essay should make clear that this is not a cynical argument. The internet did not fail. The first market story about the internet failed.

## What kind of bubble is this?

The series should distinguish between several types of bubble.

### Fraud and leverage bubbles

The 2008 financial crisis was different. It was rooted in fraud, opacity, leverage, mispriced risk, broken incentives and balance sheet rot.

That is not the emotional texture of dotcom or AI.

### Pure speculation bubbles

Tulip-like bubbles, meme bubbles and parts of crypto can become self-referential. The thing being traded matters less than the belief that someone else will pay more later.

Crypto should be mentioned only in passing. It may still become a real paradigm shift if it eventually displaces fiat currency, settlement systems or trusted financial intermediaries at scale. But so far, much of its mainstream economic role has been speculation, regulatory arbitrage, fraud, corruption, niche settlement and early access to grey or black-market use cases.

The point is not to dismiss crypto completely. The point is to say that AI is different because the utility is already obvious.

### Discovery bubbles inside real paradigm shifts

Dotcom and AI belong in this category.

A discovery bubble happens when:

1. A genuinely powerful technology appears.
2. Society can feel that it matters.
3. Nobody yet understands the mature business model.
4. Capital floods into every plausible version of the future.
5. The wrong companies are priced as if they own the right future.
6. Belief eventually breaks.
7. The system consolidates.
8. The mature paradigm emerges after the crash.

This is the pattern the series should explore.

## The sequence of predictions

The series should avoid a hard timeframe. The prediction is not date based. It is condition based.

The AI bubble bursts when several conditions converge and belief changes.

### Prediction 1: The AI bubble will burst because AI is real

The crash will not be triggered by the realization that AI is useless.

It will be triggered by the realization that AI is useful in ways that undermine the assumptions currently priced into model labs, hyperscalers, SaaS companies and AI wrappers.

The market will move from:

> These companies are expensive because they are building the future.

To:

> These companies are expensive because we misidentified who owns the future.

### Prediction 2: Intelligence as an API is a transitional phase

OpenAI, Anthropic and similar companies are betting on a world where intelligence flows through their models and infrastructure.

That is a scarcity bet.

They have the model. They have the infrastructure. They have the distribution of attention. They have the API. They have the brand.

But technology attacks scarcity.

Models will get better, smaller, cheaper, more local and more specialized. Some tasks will still require frontier cloud models, but most daily tasks will not.

The likely future is hybrid:

- local models for private, low-latency, ambient work;
- enterprise-local or tenant-local models for sensitive workflows;
- cloud frontier models for high-end reasoning, multimodal work, scientific tasks, expensive planning and specialist escalation.

The LLM API does not disappear, but it stops being the default substrate for everything.

ChatGPT may be the AOL of AI. It made the paradigm visible to normal people, but that does not guarantee it owns the mature paradigm.

### Prediction 3: SaaS is more vulnerable than the market believes

SaaS won because custom software was expensive, slow and risky.

Businesses adapted themselves to software because software could not economically adapt itself to every business.

AI changes that equation.

If AI makes custom software cheap, fast and maintainable, many SaaS categories become exposed.

This does not mean all SaaS dies. Systems of record survive longer. Regulated ledgers, finance cores, payroll, identity, tax, HR master data and deep compliance systems are harder to casually replace.

But systems of workflow are vulnerable:

- forms;
- dashboards;
- internal portals;
- approval flows;
- support workflows;
- CRM overlays;
- reporting layers;
- vertical workflow tools;
- lightweight operational systems;
- seat-based productivity tools.

Many SaaS companies are essentially database plus forms plus permissions plus workflow plus reporting plus integrations.

Historically, that was valuable because building it well was expensive.

AI lowers the madness threshold for building custom software.

### Prediction 4: AI-operated companies become a new organizational form

The real transformation is not only companies using AI.

It is companies run by AI.

The sequence is:

> AI writes software. AI operates workflows. AI coordinates departments. AI monitors markets. AI changes the software. AI runs more and more of the company.

This is the serious version of the DAO idea from crypto, but without needing crypto as the foundation.

Crypto DAOs had treasury, voting and smart contracts. They did not have operational intelligence.

AI provides the missing operating layer.

The early AI-operated company will probably be a normal legal entity with an AI operating core.

It will have a tiny human team and a large automated surface area:

- product definition;
- marketing operations;
- sales operations;
- support;
- reporting;
- pricing;
- research;
- compliance checks;
- software generation;
- workflow execution;
- monitoring;
- customer communication;
- finance admin;
- continuous process improvement.

This will put pressure on human-heavy services companies and software companies alike.

### Prediction 5: The breaking point is psychological

Bubbles do not burst the moment something is overvalued.

They burst when belief can no longer carry the valuation.

In dotcom, the internet did not stop being important. What changed was belief in the companies, business models, burn rates and implied futures that investors had funded.

For AI, the convergence may look like this:

1. Model capability keeps improving, but not fast enough to justify every valuation.
2. Local, open and specialized models reduce the scarcity premium of central APIs.
3. Enterprises discover that AI value is real, but integration is slower and harder than expected.
4. Token economics become visible once subsidies and flat-rate pricing fade.
5. Hyperscaler capex becomes harder to justify at the same growth rate.
6. SaaS companies begin to see pricing pressure, seat pressure and growth pressure.
7. Thin AI wrappers fail.
8. Investors ask who really owns the future.
9. Belief changes.

That is the convergence point.

### Prediction 6: After the crash comes the real AI economy

The crash is not the end.

It is the transition from imagination capital to operating capital.

After the dotcom crash, the internet became more real, not less real.

The open, weird, chaotic, independent early web became:

- search;
- social platforms;
- cloud;
- mobile;
- app stores;
- algorithmic feeds;
- advertising platforms;
- surveillance capitalism;
- walled gardens;
- the consumer as the product.

The mature internet was not the utopia many imagined. It was useful, profitable, centralized and extractive.

AI may follow the same pattern, but with a much deeper reach into labour, software and organizational structure.

After the AI crash:

- weak model labs disappear or are absorbed;
- infrastructure is reused;
- model access becomes cheaper;
- AI becomes embedded and invisible;
- SaaS consolidates;
- workflow software is increasingly generated;
- AI-operated companies emerge;
- enterprise control planes become critical;
- chips, devices, identity, data, governance and orchestration become strategic layers;
- the social impact becomes impossible to ignore.

The crash is when AI becomes serious.

## Series structure

### 1. The Business Cards in the Drawer

Purpose: establish the lived memory of dotcom and the general pattern of paradigm-shift bubbles.

Opening image: two packs of business cards from investor and founder events in San Francisco around 2000.

Core argument: the internet did not fail. The first market story about the internet failed.

Key examples:

- Pets.com and Chewy/Amazon pet supplies;
- online clothes shopping and Zalando/ASOS;
- the early web as a child;
- the crash as consolidation and maturation.

Ending: AI now has the same emotional texture.

### 2. The AI Bubble Will Not Burst Because AI Is Fake

Purpose: make the direct AI argument.

Core argument: AI is useful, which is precisely why the bubble is dangerous.

Distinctions:

- 2008 fraud/leverage bubble;
- crypto/speculation bubble;
- dotcom/AI discovery bubble.

Central line:

> The AI bubble will burst not because AI disappoints, but because AI succeeds in a direction the market has not priced correctly.

### 3. The False Moat of Intelligence as an API

Purpose: explain why model API companies may be overvalued even if AI succeeds.

Core argument: centralized model access is a phase, not the final form.

Key claims:

- models get smaller;
- inference gets cheaper;
- local and hybrid inference wins many daily tasks;
- model quality diffuses;
- distribution, devices, chips and data matter more than model quality alone.

Central line:

> A model lead is not a moat. It is a lead.

### 4. SaaS Was a Workaround

Purpose: explain why SaaS is exposed.

Core argument: SaaS won because custom software was too expensive. AI attacks that assumption.

Key claims:

- businesses adapted to software because software was scarce;
- AI makes software more fluid;
- systems of workflow are vulnerable;
- systems of record survive longer;
- seat-based pricing comes under pressure;
- AI features become table stakes.

Central line:

> SaaS did not win because generic software was perfect. SaaS won because custom software was too expensive.

### 5. The AI-Operated Company

Purpose: introduce the next organizational form.

Core argument: the future company may be mostly automated, with humans setting goals, constraints and approvals.

Key claims:

- AI compresses labour;
- AI compresses coordination;
- AI creates and maintains software;
- small human teams can operate large business surfaces;
- this is a serious version of the DAO idea, but with operational intelligence.

Central line:

> The next great startup may not be a company that sells AI. It may be a company that is mostly run by AI.

### 6. The Convergence Point

Purpose: explain how the bubble breaks.

Core argument: no single trigger is required. The break happens when enough assumptions fail together and belief changes.

Signals:

- capex discipline from hyperscalers;
- enterprise ROI scrutiny;
- token-cost visibility;
- SaaS growth pressure;
- AI wrapper failures;
- local model progress;
- slower model improvement relative to expectations;
- financing stress around AI infrastructure;
- investors asking who owns the future.

Central line:

> The breaking point is not technical. It is psychological.

### 7. After the Crash Comes the Real AI Economy

Purpose: explain what comes after.

Core argument: the crash is the transition to the adult phase.

Dotcom after-effects:

- consolidation;
- centralization;
- real monetization;
- walled gardens;
- consumer exploitation;
- surveillance advertising;
- platform dependency.

AI after-effects:

- embedded intelligence;
- consolidated model providers;
- cheaper inference;
- generated workflows;
- AI-operated companies;
- private data as capital;
- governance as a moat;
- labour disruption.

Central line:

> The crash is not when AI ends. It is when AI becomes boring enough to become infrastructure.

### 8. The New Power Map

Purpose: close the series by mapping likely winners and losers.

Likely winners:

- chip makers;
- memory and packaging;
- device ecosystems;
- operating systems;
- enterprise identity and permission systems;
- workflow orchestration;
- systems of record;
- observability;
- governance;
- companies with proprietary data and process knowledge.

Likely losers or pressured groups:

- pure model API companies without durable distribution;
- thin AI wrappers;
- generic SaaS workflow companies;
- seat-based SaaS vendors;
- human-heavy services companies;
- consultancies selling vague AI transformation;
- companies with no proprietary workflow or data advantage.

Final line candidate:

> The market is looking for the next software company. It should be looking for the next form of the company itself.

## Tone rules

Use first person where it matters.

Use personal memory as evidence, not as nostalgia.

Prefer concrete claims over abstract futurism.

Avoid saying “nobody knows.” The point of the series is to make a strong prediction.

Avoid false certainty about dates.

Avoid generic AI essay language:

- “rapidly evolving landscape”;
- “game changer”;
- “unlocking potential”;
- “transformative power” unless used deliberately;
- “only time will tell”;
- “it remains to be seen.”

Avoid em dashes. Use commas, colons or short sentences.

Avoid a moralizing anti-tech tone. The argument is not that AI is bad. The argument is that AI is real and the market is pricing the wrong ownership layer.

## What not to overclaim

Do not claim all SaaS dies.

Do not claim OpenAI or Anthropic disappear.

Do not claim local models replace frontier cloud models entirely.

Do not claim exact timing.

Do not claim AI-operated companies will have literally zero humans.

Do not claim crypto is worthless. It is better treated as an unfinished or still-unproven paradigm shift.

Do not turn the series into a social-impact manifesto. Labour, unemployment and civilization-scale change should be mentioned, but the main focus is business, capital markets and software economics. A later series can explore social impact in depth.

## Working one-sentence thesis

The AI bubble will burst because AI is real enough to collapse the very scarcities that current AI and SaaS valuations depend on.

## Working paragraph thesis

I believe the AI bubble will burst, but not because AI is fake. It will burst because AI is real. The market has correctly sensed a paradigm shift, but it is pricing that shift through the wrong ownership model: centralized model APIs, hyperscaler capex curves, SaaS incumbents adding AI features, and a flood of thin AI wrappers. The more AI succeeds, the more it attacks these assumptions. Models become smaller, cheaper and more local. Custom software becomes cheaper to build and maintain. Generic SaaS workflows lose scarcity. AI-operated companies emerge with tiny human teams. Eventually, these pressures converge. The technical story does not fail. The market story fails. That is when the bubble breaks.
